## Birth of Influencer Marketing

The scene is set in the late 2000s — D2C brands are realizing that with the rise of online media platforms, the younger population doesn't seem to enjoy watching television as much as they used to a few years ago. This posed a serious problem. How to advertise to them?

Moreover, televisions broadcast the same media content to everyone. Reaching viewers belonging to a specific set of buckets of interest suited to the product can be a game-changer for brands. For example, advertising whey protein to less-active viewers won't be the most profitable investment, but if the brand could advertise its product to solely fitness freaks, the cost of marketing would get slashed significantly. This would, in turn, boost the returns on investment. Hence, the need for personalized marketing arose.

Before addressing how this problem was solved — let's understand the why. Why did brands want to advertise to the younger population? The reason was quite simple: the younger folks spent more compulsively since most of them did not have any major financial commitments. Thus, the opportunity was very lucrative, but the question remained how?

The answer: Pseudo Celebs a.k.a "Influencers"

Brands hunted for famous personas on blogging platforms to endorse their products. This worked well because these bloggers already had a strong following of like-minded individuals who enjoyed their content (it could be anything: gaming, fitness, cooking, art, etc). The need to sell to millions evolved into a need to sell to a handful of influencers. These influencers could then endorse these products to their crowds, which had a terrific turnover because the influencer was a person with whom the followers correlated themselves and hence, trusted deeply.

Doubting the power of the aforementioned trust?

> According to Annalect Research's findings, among the 800 users surveyed, 49% relied on influencer recommendations and 56% relied on close friends. That's right, people are likely to trust an influencer just as much as their real-life close friends when making a purchase.

Thus, influencer marketing is an advanced form of word-of-mouth marketing, where influential figures on social media help consumers filter the brand options they come across.

While endorsements were booming, YouTube made the process even simpler. With YouTube ads, brands could advertise their products on famous vloggers' videos without needing to even collaborate with them. On the financial side, it made a lot of sense for marketers. A 100 million audience could be tapped into for <$100K. The same would've cost millions of dollars over television.

Most importantly, it helped small businesses grow by working with small influencers to directly tap into niche segments of interest buckets — a boon to bootstrapped startups.

The industry didn't settle back then and is growing rapidly to this day. Valued at $8 billion in 2019, the global influencer marketing industry is expected to grow up to $24.1 billion in 2025 with a CAGR of 20.1%.

Seems too rosy to be true, right? Well, it's not like influencer marketing is free from drawbacks.

## The Emergence of a Bubble?

Let's first answer what a bubble is. A bubble is a situation in which prices are much higher than the reasonably justifiable "true worth" of the asset. Bubbles can be caused by overly optimistic projections about the future — prices which strongly exceed the asset's intrinsic value.

The whole set-up of influencer marketing relies on a single human emotion: **TRUST**. How do you quantitatively measure trust? Taking it a level deeper, how do you numerically assess the value added to a brand's awareness by an influencer? While some metrics can be defined to do so, it is impossible to track with high accuracy — since these influencer campaigns are detached from sales, unlike targeted ads. This is where the case of a bubble arises.

### Reason 1: Inability to Track the True Worth of Influencer Marketing

Human emotions cannot be broken down into 0s and 1s. While there is the ever-present risk of overvaluing assets, the risk deepens when the asset in question is something as abstract as trust.

Some might say that the growth in followers or likes could be used as a metric — and up to an extent, yes, but it isn't reliable at all. Consider the case of Instagram influencer Arii, real name Arianna Renee. She had 2 million followers on Instagram and was asked to sell just 36 T-shirts as an assessment of her reach. The marketing stunt failed and the handful of buyers were reimbursed.

The failure was twofold:
- There was a lack of genuine engagement between her and her followers
- The T-shirt's design didn't complement her personality — so followers couldn't identify why they should buy it in the first place

This proves that follower count alone cannot predict the success of influencer marketing.

Quick fact: engagement can be created artificially. An online retailer, A Good Company, worked with 4,000 influencers to promote its eco-friendly products and didn't get its expected sales boost. An anonymous survey sent to their influencers found that nearly two-thirds had paid for followers, likes or comments.

> When you pay for a billboard, you can estimate how many people would have seen it. With Instagram, you simply cannot, because engagement can be bought.

HypeAuditor, an analytics firm, investigated 1.84 million Instagram accounts and found over 50% used fraud to inflate followers — comprising inactive accounts and bots. These scams cost little: click farms sell 1,000 bogus YouTube followers for $49, Instagram followers for just $16.

### Reason 2: It Isn't Cheap Anymore

Influencer Marketing's massive upside led to popularity, and many companies flocked to influencers for endorsements — causing steep rises in competition and demand. Economics 101: when demand rises, prices rise.

Earlier, small agile businesses experimented with this marketing model. Now, big businesses backed by investor capital dominate the space. Some firms with enough investment don't seem to care whether revenue upside exceeds expenditure — because investors were more focused on growth than profitability.

However, investment appetite has been declining along with weakening investor interest in influencer marketing owing to high-profile failures. Yet the fact that nearly two-thirds of marketers plan to increase their influencer marketing budgets has led to talks of a bubble.

### Reason 3: Erosion of Trust

Remember the critical need for trust for this system to sustain? Consumer trust has been on the decline because most influencers seem to care more about making money than vouching for products they genuinely like. Many influencers endorse just anything that pays well.

The whole industry was born on the premise that people find themselves similar to influencers and thus trust their recommendations. But now that influencers are promoting things different from their own personalities, followers are losing interest — finding every recommendation to be a marketing stunt.

Further, influencer scams are ruining the public image of the industry. Many influencers have started targeting kids for promotions. For example, Jake Paul once posted a 13-minute video whose 7 minutes were just upselling products to kids. But down the years, even this tactic will become less effective, because the young adults who are aware of how influencer endorsements work will become the parents of those kids.

Many businesses are also wary because their reputations are interlinked with influencers. One mistake and the brand could lose its credibility along with the influencer — making influencers riskier than traditional celebrity endorsements.

## So… Is This a Bubble or Not?

This one is a tricky question.

Does influencer marketing actually positively impact a brand's sales and public image? Yes, it does. When leveraged properly, social media influencers can bring benefits to a brand: authentic content creation, brand awareness, and performance reporting on metrics beyond audience size.

But the sad reality is that very few influencer campaigns are planned with well-defined expectations and measurable outcomes. We might see a correction in the costs that big brands incur on influencers. But one thing is certain: even if there is a bubble and it does pop, it won't be the end of the industry. The few influencers who endorse only products they genuinely like will continue to thrive — because they sustain the long-standing trust of their followers and wouldn't sway for more money.

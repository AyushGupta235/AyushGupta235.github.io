## The Problem with Impulse Spending

What is Buyer's Remorse? Buyer's Remorse is the sense of regret after having made a purchase. It is frequently linked with the purchase of luxurious items such as a new car, a flagship smartphone, or a new refrigerator. These unplanned expenditures typically skyrocket during festive seasons, when splurging money on expensive items feels good while shopping, but the feeling soon turns into remorse.

According to a study in India, over 64% of people regret buying products during sales or limited offers and end up rarely using them. There exists a strong correlation between the prevalence of offers and discounts and an increase in regret-causing impulse purchases.

New age apps featuring Buy Now, Pay Later (BNPL) payments have exploited this innate human desire for instant gratification and have pushed people into taking more and more credit — and after a point, their debt balloons and they're stuck in vicious debt-traps.

> **BNPL:** Buy Now Pay Later payments are like very short-term loans for small purchases. They allow customers to purchase something and pay for it later, often interest-free for a certain period.
>
> **Debt-traps:** When someone takes up a lot of loans, the interest payments become so high that their income cannot repay them — forcing them to take out loans to repay existing loans.

## Enter Save Now, Buy Later

Amidst all this chaos of rising impulse spending, enters a new variety of FinTechs called **Save Now, Buy Later (SNBL)** platforms. These platforms promote planned expenditures using savings, most popular among users under the age of 35. They encourage the principle of goal-based investing.

During the pandemic, many investors started investing in stocks, mutual funds, or crypto without any goal in mind other than making money. Without a goal, it is difficult to define the amount to invest, the duration, or the type of assets to invest in.

India has always had the tradition of prioritizing savings, with credit card penetration of less than 3%. Methods of buying on credit are available to a niche urban segment only. However, their savings typically lie idle in bank accounts where they are losing out to inflation.

> If inflation is 5% and savings deposits earn 4% interest, your money is losing value by 1% annually. Whereas if invested in a stock earning 8% returns, your money's value rises by 3%.

SNBL platforms are essentially Robo-advisors that offer wealth management services based on spending goals. Users pay for the product or experience they desire — a car, a vacation — in advance through Equated Monthly Installments (EMIs), in return for discounts and cashback. The market for such tools has grown to Rs. 1,500 crores ($181 million). Some well-known SNBL platforms are Multipl, Hubble, and Tortoise, with Multipl registering ~10x growth to 300,000 users in the past 12 months.

These platforms assist users in two primary ways:

- **Hubble** provides a gift card for the cumulative amount — the money deposited plus the incentive
- **Tortoise** transfers the money back to the user's account and offers 10% cashback when the invoice for the purchase is shared
- **Multipl** allows users to invest in AI-devised mutual fund schemes that can generate returns and lower the overall cost of the purchase

Their main premise: make savings a habit and offer incentives via tie-ups with brands. They earn their share of profits via revenue-sharing partnerships with these brands.

## What's in It for Merchants?

- No need to pay a cut to credit providers, unlike BNPL platforms
- Capture customers early when they're planning a big purchase — broadening the customer acquisition funnel
- Buyer's remorse decreases dramatically, since customers have been saving for the purchase for months without compromising their income
- Customers locked in early can be upsold to — increasing revenue per customer

## SNBL vs BNPL

**BNPL** emphasizes obtaining credit and receiving instant satisfaction. In the absence of a proper savings plan, this can result in debt traps, hurt credit scores, and lower savings for emergencies. The rigidity of repayment schedules, interest costs, and processing fees makes it expensive — sometimes interest costs can reach 15–20% if the repayment schedule is disturbed.

**SNBL** scores over BNPL in terms of accessibility — anyone can use it, even those without a credit score, since there is no prior approval process. It offers a debt-free alternative to high-value purchases, doesn't impact credit score, and provides rewards on top of zero-cost EMIs.

But BNPL has its advantages too, especially for those who need to make an immediate purchase but are running low on cash. SNBL is better suited for major planned expenses, while BNPL bodes well with small, immediate ones.

## The Road Ahead

The growth of SNBL startups coincides with the post-Covid era, which saw an inherent increase in awareness around savings. The challenge that needs to be overcome is onboarding enough merchants so that more customers can be attracted toward saving in lieu of discounts and cashback.

The underlying shift is cultural: SNBL is betting that a generation burned by debt will want a different relationship with money — one where the reward comes after the discipline, not before.

## "A jack-of-all-trades is a master of none, but oftentimes better than a master of one."

### Two Worlds: Kind and Wicked

Heard of the story of Tiger Woods? He started playing with a putter (a golf club) at the young age of 7 MONTHS. He won a ten-and-under golf tournament at the age of TWO. Later, Woods went on to become arguably one of the greatest golf players of all time.

Same is the case with the Polgar sisters - their father trained and developed them in chess from a very early age. They went onto beat national champions at the shallow ages of 8-10. Soon enough, all 3 of them were few of the top players internationally. All thanks to their father's focus on specializing them early on.

After these type of stories became folklore - many started suggesting that early specialization is the key to success. If thousands of kids were specialized in various fields, humanity could solve the problems that are perceived to be unsolvable.

But there is one important and unspoken assumption made in these claims: all activities, medicine, law, engineering, entrepreneurship, etc. present learning environments similar to that of chess and golf, and can be mastered through consistent and deliberate practice (which is actually false)

#### Kind Learning Environments

Gary Klein has shown that experts in certain fields are remarkably similar to chess masters in that they instinctively recognize familiar patterns

Greatest chess players see a chain of moves, a combination, instantly whenever their opponents play a piece in a given situation

Firefighters make 80% of their decisions almost instinctively in seconds, identifying patterns in behavior of flames and buildings on the verge of collapse

#### Wicked Learning Environments

Kahneman, through a study on human decision making, discovered that experience had not helped many highly trained experts at all. Even worse, it frequently bred confidence but not skill.

Financial markets can't be mastered through deliberate practice → that would need one to know ALL the facts, ALL the time and the market must react in the same way

Starting up or leading a conglomerate is impossible to master through repeated practice

No one can build the perfect software, bugs always arise and take time to solve

#### Differences between the two cases

The two started a collaborative research to identify whether specialists get better with experience, and the answer is, it depends on the domain in question.

Domains that Klein studied were "kind environments"

Patterns repeat over and over, and feedback is extremely accurate and rapid

In golf or chess, the ball/pieces move within certain boundaries and rules only → the consequences of the same are quickly apparent

> The learning environment is kind because a learner improves simply by engaging in the activity and trying to do better.

Kahneman focused on the flip side of kind environments: wicked learning environments

Such environments have a broader scope of learning, with usually infinite possible outcomes of the same actions

The impact that you make in a large firm, is learned only during the feedback period which would take place, say once in a quarter, and your individual impact can rarely be quantified into business metrics

> In wicked environments, rules of the game are often unclear or incomplete, there may or may not be repetitive patterns, and there may or may not be obvious feedback, which may also be delayed or inaccurate or both

💡 **Moravec's Paradox:** Machines and humans frequently have opposite strengths & weaknesses

Machine Learning excels in kind learning environments, since the main premise of ML is repeated practice. That is why computers are far better at chess than humans. But the same cannot be said for building a viral product or defining the laws of a country.

💡 **Cognitive entrenchment:** When kind environment experts such as chess players, poker players, accountants, etc. are exposed to slightly altered rules, such as a different rule for moving a piece or a new tax law for deductions → Experts perform even worse than novices.

To avoid the same, the suggestion is the polar opposite of the fabled 10,000 hours practice rule: vary challenges within a domain drastically and insist on "having one foot outside of your world"

### Why the Wicked World exists

💡 **Flynn Effect:** The increase in correct IQ test (Raven's Progressive Matrices) answers with each new generation. The gains are typically 3 points every 10 years i.e. an average World War I soldier (at 50th percentile) would fall at the 22nd percentile when compared to World War II soldiers

Today we see the world through "scientific spectacles". Rather than relying on our own direct experiences, we make sense of reality through classification schemes, using layers of abstract concepts to understand how pieces of information relate to one another

Modern work demands knowledge transfer: the ability to apply knowledge to new situations and different domains. Our fundamental thought processes accommodate increasing complexity and the need to derive new patterns instead of relying only on familiar ones

The more constrained and repetitive a challenge, the more likely that it will be automated, while greater rewards will accrue to those who acquire knowledge from one domain and apply it in an entirely new one

> Based on another one of Flynn's studies, the correlation between the scores of a test on broad conceptual thinking and GPA was about zero. In Flynn's words, "The traits that earn good grades at universities do not include critical ability of any broad significance"

The more "contexts" in which something is learned, the more abstract models the learner creates, and less they rely on any particular example

Learners become better at applying knowledge to situations they've never seen before, which is the essence of creativity

Based on an MRI scan of a few of the famous musicians of the past: brain areas associated with focused attention, inhibition, and self-censoring turned down when the musicians were creating new material. They do the opposite of consciously identifying errors and stopping to correct them → acquire the skill first, then learn the rules of applying it

Parents with creative children made their opinions known to their kids about the things they didn't like after the kids did it, they just did not prescribe it beforehand. Their households were low on prior restraint → kids tried out more and more things

### Learning, Fast and Slow

#### Two Learning Methods equipped by Teachers

Using procedures: Practice at something that was just learned. For example, take the formula of the sum of interior angles of a polygon is 180\*(n-2) and apply it on a worksheet

Making connections: Connecting concepts to a broader concept. For example, asking why the formula works in the first place

#### Improving long term learning (applicable as both, teacher and student)

Desirable difficulties: Obstacles that make learning more challenging, slower, and more frustrating in the shorter term, but better in the longer term (like me writing this summary :p)

Excessive hint-giving does the opposite: it bolsters immediate performance, but undermines progress in the long run

Generation Effect: Struggling to generate an answer on YOUR OWN, even a wrong one, enhances subsequent learning

Hypercorrection Effect: The more confident a learner is of their wrong answer, the better the information sticks when they learn the correct answer. Tolerating big mistakes can create the best learning opportunities.

Spacing or Distributed Practice: Leave time between practice sessions for the same material

aka deliberate not-practicing between bouts of deliberate practice

Space between practices creates the hardness that enhances learning

Interleaving: Varied or mixed practice to ensure flexible knowledge

Improves inductive reasoning

When presented with different examples mixed together, students learn to create abstract generalizations that allow them to apply what they learned to problems they had never seen before

### Thinking Outside Experience

Human intuition is not very well engineered to make use of the best tools when faced with what researchers called "ill-defined problems". Our experience-based instincts are set up well for Tiger domains, where problems and solutions repeat

Not using more than one analogy does not help battle the natural impulse to employ the "inside view". We take the inside view when we make judgements narrowly based on the details of a particular project that is right in front of us.

If asked to predict whether a particular horse will win the race, the more internal details you learn about the horse, say its physical qualities, the more likely you are to say that the scenario you are investigating will occur

The more internal details an individual can be made to consider, the more extreme their judgement becomes

The more unusual the challenge, the more distant the analogies, moving away from surface similarities and toward deep structural similarities

### Match Quality

> "Never give in, never, never, never, never, except to convictions of honor and good sense" -Winston Churchill

Career switchers are winners, which seems to fly in the face of hoary adages about quitting.

No one in their right mind would argue that passion and perseverance are unimportant, or that a bad day is a cue to quit. But the idea that a change of interest or recalibration of focus, is an imperfection and competitive disadvantage leads to a simple one-size-fits-all Tiger Woods story: pick and stick ASAP.

Responding to lived experience with a change of direction is less tidy but no less important. It involves a particular behavior that improves the chances of finding the best career match, but that at first blush sounds like a terrible life strategy: short-term planning.

Many great leaders of the past used the shorthand "standardization covenant" for the cultural notion that it is rational to trade a winding path of self-exploration for a rigid goal with a head start because it ensures stability

The people who are fulfilled do pursue a long-term goal, but they only formulate it after a period of discovery. It's actually riskier to make a commitment before you know how it fits you

Career goals that once felt safe and certain can appear ludicrous when examined in light of more self-knowledge

Our work preferences and our life preferences do not stay the same, because we do not stay the same

#### End of history illusion

When asked to do two things separately; predicting how much you would change in the next decade, and reflecting how much you've changed in the past decade; predictors expect very little change in them while reflectors report that they have changed a lot.

💡 The most momentous personality changes occur between age 18 and late-20s, so specializing early is a task of predicting match-quality for a person who does not yet exist.

To better assess individuals on their "grit", instead of asking whether someone is gritty, we should ask when they are. Individuals have different match-fits, and hence, are more committed to causes that fit better to them

We discover our own possibilities by doing, by trying new activities, building new networks, finding new role models. We learn who we are in practice, not in theory

Rather than a grand plan, find experiments that can be undertaken quickly. "Test-and-learn", "not plan-and-implement"

💡 **Outside-in Thinking:** Finding solutions in experiences far outside of focused training for the problem itself. The trick is to seek outside help on highly specialized problems to facilitate outside-of-the-box thinking and analogical reasoning: framing the challenge in such a way that it attracts a diverse array of solvers. The more likely a challenge is to appeal to scientists but also to attorneys and dentists and mechanics, the more likely it is to be solved.

The more information specialists create, the more opportunity exists for curious generalists to contribute by merging strands of widely available but disparate information - undiscovered public knowledge.

### Lateral Thinking with Withered Technology

💡 **Lateral Thinking:** Reimagining of information in new contexts, including drawing together of seemingly disparate concepts or domains that can give old ideas new uses. **Withered Technology:** Tech that is old enough to be extremely well understood and easily available, so it doesn't require a specialist's knowledge.

Why and how does this help an individual, when there are big corporations and tech giants working at the cutting-edge of technology?

Withered technology has wide availability and doesn't suffer from various prevalent issues such as low internet penetration, silicon shortage, smartphone unavailability, etc.

There is well-documented tendency people have to consider only familiar uses for objects, an instinct known as functional fixedness.

Gameboy by Nintendo had a monochrome screen (same as the ones on calculators) and it was launched at a time when competitors like Sega and Atari were launching gamepads with colored displays

While the other handheld consoles were technologically far superior, the Gameboy crushed them, what it lacked in tech, made up in user experience

- It was cheap
- It could fit in a large pocket
- It was indestructible, only a horrific drop could crack its screen - and it would keep ticking
- If it went into the washing machine, dry it for a few days and it's running again
- Old hardware was extremely familiar to developers - creative games pumped in

> "If you draw two circles on a blackboard, and say, 'That's a snowman', everyone who sees it will sense the white color of the snow" - Gunpei Yokoi, Inventor of Gameboy

Specialist contributions sky-rocketed around and after WWII, but more recently have declined. Specialists specifically peaked around 1985, and then declined pretty dramatically, levelled off about 2007, and recently have been on the decline again

Why? As information becomes more broadly available (thanks to the internet), the need for somebody to just advance a field isn't as critical because in effect they are available to everyone

Communication has limited the number of specialists required to work on a particular narrow problem, because their breakthrough can be communicated quickly and widely to others - the generalists - who work on clever applications

They are still absolutely critical, it's just that their work is highly accessible, so fewer suffice.

#### Mosaic Building - Learning as a Generalist

Try to be a "T-shaped person", one who has breadth compared to an "I-shaped person", who only goes deep. T-people can go to I-people with questions to create the trunk of the "T"

To attack a problem, start by building a narrative. Figure out the fundamental questions to ask, and if you ask those questions of the people who actually know their stuff, you are still exactly where you would be if you had all this knowledge inherently

This works well because as ambiguity and uncertainty increases, which is the norm in the wicked world, breadth becomes increasingly important

💡 Individuals are more capable of more creative integration of diverse experiences than teams are. Diverse experience is impactful when created by platoon in teams, and even more impactful when contained within an individual.

In kind environments, where the goal is to recreate prior performance with as little deviation as possible, teams of specialists work superbly. For example, surgeons and flight crews, all want the same outcome with minimal surprises, and hence, specialized groups thrive here.

### Fooled by Expertise - Forecasting the Future

There is a particular kind of thinker, nicknamed "Hedgehog", who becomes more entrenched in their single big idea about how the world works in the face of contrary facts, whose predictions become worse, not better, as they amass information for their mental representation of the world.

> Seeing small pieces of a large jigsaw puzzle in isolation, no matter how hi-def the picture, is insufficient to tackle humanity's greatest challenges. We have long known the laws of thermodynamics, but struggle to predict the flow of a forest fire. We have a thorough understanding of the human cell, but fail the predict the poetry written by the human composed of the very same cells. A frog's eye view of individual parts at the ground level is not enough. A healthy ecosystem needs biodiversity.

The best forecasters are foxes with dragonfly eyes. Dragonfly eyes are composed of thousands of lenses, each with a different perspective, which are then synthesized in the dragonfly's brain.

> The best forecasters see their ideas as hypotheses in need of testing. Their aim is not to convince their teammates of their expertise, but to encourage them to help falsify their notions

💡 Beneath complexity, hedgehogs tend to see simple, deterministic rules of cause and effect framed by their area of expertise, like repeating patterns on a chess board. Foxes see complexity in what others mistake for simple cause and effect. They understand that most cause-and-effect relationships are probabilistic, not deterministic.

When an outcome on their prediction takes them by surprise, foxes are much likely to adjust their ideas. Some hedgehogs make authoritative predictions that turn out wildly wrong, and then update their theories in the wrong direction. They become even more convinced of the original beliefs that led them astray.

> This is called, in a word: learning. Sometimes, it involves putting experience aside entirely

### Building Better Organizations - Learning to Drop Familiar Tools

Many organizations have a "can do" attitude that focuses on following rigid systems to solve the most complex challenges → this needs to be evolved to a "make do" attitude, improvise rather than throwing out information that does not fit the established rubric

💡 **Overlearned behavior:** Doing the same thing to the same challenges over and over again until the behavior has become so automatic that organizations no longer recognize it as a situation-specific tool. For eg, NASA's infamous Challenger disaster.

A leadership should not be a "decision-making" body, but a "sensemaking" one. They should employ "hunches held lightly"; giving decisive directions to the team, but with transparent rationale and the addendum that the plan is ripe for revision as the team collectively makes sense of a problem.

#### Culture Fit - Congruence Craze

💡 **Congruence:** A social science term for cultural "fit" among an institution's components - values, goals, vision, self-concepts, and leadership styles.

Based on a study of 334 institutions of higher education, it was found that cultural congruence had no influence on any measure of organizational structure whatsoever. Most effective organizations and leaders often had range; they were, in effect, paradoxical

Experiments show that an effective problem solving culture maintained a balanced standard culture - this was done by expanding the organization's range by identifying the dominant culture and diversifying it by pushing in the opposite direction

Consensus is considered to be essential in team-building, while it is nice to have, we should not be optimizing happiness, we should be optimizing decisions

> Teams need elements of both, hierarchy and individualism, to succeed. It's not easy, but critical.

Most importantly for an organization, the chain of communication should be informal, which must be completely different from the chain of command.

Instead of a ladder, an organization should be structured as concentric circles - information should flow in all directions, with every person having multiple entry points of communication to the next level, and not just one superior person as the gate.

In networks that acted as fertile soil for successful groups, individuals moved smoothly within teams, crossing organizational boundaries and discovering new collaborators. Unsuccessful teams, on the other hand, were broken into isolated clusters where the same people collaborated over and over. Efficient and comfortable, perhaps, but not a creative engine

### Expanding your Range

If this book was to be summarized into one sentence: Don't feel behind.

Everyone learns at their own pace, so don't let anyone else make you feel behind. You probably don't even know where exactly you're going, so feeling behind doesn't help.

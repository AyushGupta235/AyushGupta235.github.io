*Human Behaviour · Behavioural Economics · Neuroscience*

When we make prompt judgements based on resemblance, we often forgo the consideration of statistics (for eg, just because someone is neat and tidy, we're inclined to say they're a librarian even though farmers far outweigh librarians in number)

Availability Heuristic causes people to believe an event/fact to be more important because an example comes to mind easily (due to recency / media attention for interest, etc)

Systematic errors in human thinking are due to the nature of cognition, not a corruption by emotions. Moreover, all intuitive judgements are not made using Heuristics. More so for experts for whom prolonged practice is more at play
> Practice leads to facts being stored in memory
> Some situations provide cues → cues initiate access to info in memory → info gives answer
> With increasing skill, demand for energy decreases
> "Intuition is nothing more and nothing less than recognition"

Affect Heuristic leads to errors in judgement where people's emotions (likings and dislikes) play a role in Decision Making without deliberate thinking

- System 1 operates automatically and quickly, with little or no effort and no sense of voluntary control
- System 2 allocates attention to the effortful mental activities that demand it, including complex computations, and operations associated with Focus and concentration - Dual System Theory
- System 1 learns associations b/w ideas and picked reading and social cues. Stored in Memory and accessed w/o intention or effort. Multitasking is possible for simple n easy tasks which do not have a cognitive load
  - It reacts promptly when urgent action is needed
- System 2 > System 1 when model of world is violated - slow thinking overcomes impulse of System 1 via the expense of Attention → essential to shift focus (**paying** attention)
  - Attention is limited → anything that occupies your working memory reduces Cognitive Ability

System 2 can adopt "task sets" by investing Attention, basically overriding habitual patterns to follow an instruction, and hence, task switching is effortful, especially under time pressure

Pupils dilate when mental effort is used, dilation ∝ difficulty. People effectively become blind when in high focus

Prefrontal Cortex is associated with intelligence - more developed in humans than other primates, but the ability to control Attention is not a measure of intelligence (efficiency of controlling attention by air traffic controllers goes well beyond just intelligence)

- Self-control and deliberate thought draw on the same limited budget of effort (so do all voluntary acts of effort - cognitive, emotional, physical)
- Maintaining a train of thought and engaging in effort require self-control → people do not enjoy frequent task-switching (Law of Least Effort)

**Flow:** "a state of effortless concentration so deep that they lose their sense of time, of themselves, of their problems,"

Cognitively busy people are more selfish, and may act against to social norms when in focus, but cognitive busyness is not the only cause of weakened self-control (fatigue, alcohol, etc)

**Ego Depletion:** Efforts of will and self-control are tiring → after being forced to work on a tough task, people lose control due to fatigue. Intuitive errors are more common in ego-depleted people

The nervous system consumes maximum glucose in the human body → Ego Depletion can be reversed by consumption of glucose

- System 2 exerts control over the "suggestions" of System 1, allowing some but blocking others
  - More intelligent people have better representations of the world. Intelligence is not just the capability to reason, but also the ability to access memory to find info and deploy Attention when needed
  - Memory is an attribute of System 1
  - Slow thinkers search deeper into their memory and validate the intuitive suggestion of System 1 → the extent of deliberate checking and search is an attribute of System 2
- People who can exhibit self-control and exhibit resistance have higher control in cognitive tasks owing to better allocation of Attention
- There is a close connection between ability to control attention and controlling emotions among children
- Rationality should be distinguished from intelligence
  - Superficial or "lazy" thinking is a flaw in the reflective mind, a failure of Rationality
  - The lazy thinking tests are better indicators of our susceptibility to cognitive errors than intelligence
- Some lean towards System 1 (Lazy Thinking), some towards System 2

When prompted with a word, System 1 quickly uses connections of world view linked by Memory, emotions, etc, triggering many ideas coherently - Associative Thinking - and resulting in a reinforcing pattern of cognitive, emotional, and physical responses

- Association is linked via either resemblance, contiguity in time and place or causality
- An idea evokes multiple ideas simultaneously and not one, but only a few register in our consciousness; most of Associative Thinking is silent
- **Ideomotor Effect:** An idea can cause Cognitive Priming which can influence actions

- The mental model of the world or world view is constructed via **association of ideas or events that co-occur**. With time, this pattern represents the structure of events in our lives and shapes our expectations
- After experiencing one event, a reoccurrence doesn't follow similar reaction as the **event has already been registered and is processed in conjunction to the first occurrence**
- When our world view is violated, System 1 reacts to this abnormality with astonishing speed
- When presented with a story, our System 1 creates causal relationships derived from its world views, and upon deliberate consideration, System 2 accepts them if they're logically plausible

### "Jumping to conclusion"

When the mind makes a quick judgement, recent event and current context has the most impact, and when not present, more distant events take precedence. Such judgments are made while discarding the alternatives. System 1 doesn't store them as it requires extra mental effort

System 2 is lazy, but is doubtful and thinks critically, but System 1 is gullible, which is why tired and depleted people are easily influenced

**Positive Test Strategy:** Deliberating looking for confirming data that validates our hypothesis that is undertaken by System 2

The tendency to like (or dislike) everything about a person—including things you have not observed—is known as the **Halo Effect.**

First impressions are important - they shape up the subsequent accumulation of information such that the original judgements are validated
- **WYSIATI** - What you see is all there is
- People assume that their limited knowledge about a subject is exhaustive and make the systemic error of basing their decisions on this limited knowledge

To derive the most useful information from multiple sources of evidence, you should always try to make these sources independent of each other.

It is the consistency of the information that matters for a good story, not its completeness

### How Judgments Happen

System 1 continuously monitors everything going on inside and outside. It generates assessments without efforts

People judge competence by combining the two dimensions of strength and trustworthiness. The faces that exude competence combine a strong chin with a slight confident-appearing smile

- System 1 assesses through similarity, causality, associations and exemplars. Done in the absence of tasks, but show up to meet task demands
- System 1 does well with exemplars, and hence, average operations but not sum operations (or sum-like variables)
- Emotions prevail over quantity or size of impact when piquing generosity

When we intentionally think about something, we often overdo the thinking over what we want or need - aka mental shotgun

### The Law of Small Numbers

Extreme outcomes show up in smaller samples simply because their more likely due to smaller number of total outcomes. There is no explanation, any extremity in a small group is owing to random sampling, not causation

Given the limited sample size, we are prone to fall into the trap of exaggerating and building associative coherence - scientists fall prey to Halo Effect. By following our intuition, we justify randomness as being systemic by building reasoning to justify an event

**Content > Reliability:** We make this mistake when looking at data, and hence, jumping to conclusions is safer in imagination than reality

### Anchoring Bias

System 2 estimates by slowly adjusting away from the anchor in steps and prematurely stops at the near edge of uncertainty of the true range. This is effortful and hence, mentally depleted people stay close to anchors

System 1 is gullible and upon hearing a number, the selective activation of compatible thoughts leads to systemic error in judgement where System 1 tries to build a world view that justifies the anchor
- Random anchors are just as effective as informative anchors
- Moving first is advantageous in single-issue negotiations
- Anchoring affects System 2 and judgement tasks are completed by it
  - But System 2 works on retrieved memory that is done by System 1
  - Selective activation of coherent thoughts by System 1 creates a bias in the judgement of System 2
- System 2 has no awareness of it being influenced by anchors and denies it

### Science of Availability Heuristic

Like Heuristics in general, availability works by substituting an estimate with a simpler question that comes to mind with ease

People overestimate their contribution in a group/couple setting and the contributions of all amount to more than 100%. Issues of credit rewarding are solved by knowing this fact. Feeling you've done more in a group is the same across all the members

Difficulty in coming up with instances of our strengths has a negative impact on opinions of ourselves - people asked to come with 6 instances of assertiveness rate themselves as more assertive v/s those asked to think of 12

### Emotional Evaluation of Risk

Emotional evaluations and bodily reactions towards events contribute towards decision making

If people are presented with benefits of a tech without its risks, or low risks of a tech without its benefits, people always assume the counterpart of risk/benefit to also be positive: "The emotional tail wags the rational dog."

#### Experts v/s Public: Policymaking
- Experts measure risk in terms of lives lost or life-years lost - it is just a statistic, merely a "count"
- The public, on the other hand, draws finer distinction b/w good and bad deaths, accidents v/s voluntary acts of risk-taking (skiing)
- The public has a richer sense of risk and hence, experts should not alone build policies - Slovic

**Sunstein: "All heuristics are equal, but availability is more equal than the others"** - availability provides a heuristic for judgements other than just frequency of occurrence

Biases flow into policy through **Availability Cascade**, a self-sustaining chain of events where a minor event catches the eye of a small group, resonates with media focus and grows into a public panic (often exaggerated), eventually leading to large-scale government action

Combining **Probability Neglect**, the ignorance of real probability of occurrence in favour of availability, and Availability Cascade leads to gross exaggeration of minor threats and leads to important consequences

### Representativeness

Focusing on plain descriptions of similarity for an event and ignorance of **Base Rates**, the ground probability of something happening, regardless of credibility of the description is Representativeness

Sins of Representativeness:
1. Excessive willingness to predict less likely (low base-rate) events
   - Even when base rates are provided to participants explicitly, they still weigh individual descriptions of the case more than the statistics
2. Insensitivity to quality and reliability of evidence

**To mitigate doubts on quality of evidence, stay close to base rates when estimating risk of different events**

### Probabilities and Statistics

Conjunction Fallacy happens when people judge the combination of two events (someone being bank teller and feminist) to be more likely than one of the two (being bank teller)

Most representative outcomes of descriptions build coherent stories, and coherence is associated with plausibility by the unwary mind instead of probability

Intuition governs judgments in the between-subjects condition; logic rules in joint evaluation

Statistical base rates speak about composition of a population in general and don't affect inference of an individual case, causal base rates (85% of accidents involve green cabs) change view of how a case came to be

We categorise people into social categories called stereotypes. Stereotypes can be harmful and have bad consequences, but they're a psychological fact: that's how we think of categories

People's unwillingness to deduce the particular from the general is matched only by their willingness to infer the general from the particular

**Regression to the Mean:** The tendency of a random variable (or an event) to tend towards average performance after a particularly extreme case

Scolding is assumed to work well (causal interpretation), but in reality, the performance (or luck) was so bad that it attracted scolding and the subsequent improvement is only due to statistical reasons of Regression to the Mean - same with the notion of praise, people attach causation to statistical randomness

Whenever the correlation between two scores is imperfect, there will be regression to the mean

To assess effectiveness of any treatment, make two groups, treated and control group. Control group will improve due to regression, but treated group should improve more if it works

### Intuitive Predictions

Intuitive predictions are insensitive to quality and evidence, they're built by comprehending facts through coherent stories - WYSIATI - but they tend to overconfident and extreme

##### Dealing with intuitive bias
- Estimate base rate measure of the metric
- Estimate intuitive prediction on the basis of the given evidence
- Evaluate the correlation between the two scores
- Adjust towards prediction from the base rate in proportion of the correlation

### Illusions

The core illusion of understanding the past is that we think we know the past and can predict the future, but in reality we don't the past as well as we think

We cannot reconstruct past beliefs, particularly when the beliefs change, and it becomes difficult for us to acknowledge the surprises from the past - **Hindsight Bias.** Because of Halo Effect we often reverse causal relationships and blame a CEO for bad performance of the company when they, in reality, have little role to play

It is wise to take uncertainty seriously, lot of confidence is explained by a coherent story that may not be true

##### Finance Industry and Illusion of Skill

Financial traders are experts during uncertain situations, but in general their decisions are just as good as blind guesses. It is not an individual aberration but in the culture of the industry - people are not ready to accept what questions their personal experiences and most importantly, their livelihood

Skill in evaluation of business prospects does not answer whether info of business is included in price of stock, something traders struggle with and ignore

The more famous the forecaster, the more flamboyant the forecasts

### Intuitions Vs. Formulas

- Experts are often inferior to formulas because they try to look clever and overthink outside of the box. Formulas are simpler and offer better results. When given scores from formula, experts still do worse because they try to reject it by saying they have more information
- Experts are inconsistent too, they give different opinions for the same thing on different occasions
- Some research suggests formulas that assign equal weights to all the predictors are often superior, because they are not affected by accidents of sampling

Argument against formulas is that critical mistakes due to simple formulas will cause severe emotional responses in contrast to human errors

Intuition adds value only after a disciplined collection of objective information and disciplined scoring of separate traits decided beforehand
1. Select a few traits (max 6) that are independent from each other
2. Make a list of **factual questions**
3. Make a list of questions for each trait and think how to evaluate the trait
4. Have an idea of what being "weak" and "strong" in the trait means

### Expert Intuition

**Recognition-Primed Decision (RPD):** Decisions made by first associative relation by System 1 to come up with a plan, followed by System 2 checking and tuning the plan by deliberate thinking

Emotional learning is quick, but developing expert intuition takes deliberate practice. When can an expert's intuition can be trusted?
- When the learning environment is a kind one
- Prolonged practice to learn the skills

Skilled professionals in wicked environments are often good at short term anticipation but wrong when making long term predictions

Human thinking is normally efficient when cues are strong, but in noisy environments, statistical algorithms do better as they can detect weak but valid cues which humans may not notice

To elicit information from a group, collect opinions confidentially before the meetings instead of initiating a public discussion

### Outside View

Pallid statistics are often ignored when it does not associate with the inside view of someone with regards to something: outside view < inside view

**Planning Fallacy** causes errors of forecasting the best-case scenario, which can be fixed by incorporating statistics into thinking. Failures in forecasting resulting from ineffective reflection of own choices in the future

#### Flyvbjerg's forecasting method
- Identify appropriate reference class (building a railway network, kitchen, etc)
- Obtain statistics related to class (cost per mile of train lines, bridge, etc)
- Adjust the base predictions on the basis of specific information of the case

### Optimism

People tend to be overly optimistic about their relative standing on any activity in which they do moderately well

**Competition Neglect:** It causes excess entry, more than sustainable competitors enter the market which causes losses - can be terrible for individuals but a net positive for the economy - **Optimistic Martyrs**

Perform **premortem** when making an important decision: Call all knowledgable people and start the speeches on "Imagine that we are a year into the future. We implemented the plan as it now exists. The outcome was a disaster. Please take 5 to 10 minutes to write a brief history of that disaster."

**Theory-Induced Blindness:** The inability to think in opposition to a theory you've accepted to be true and the tendency to give the benefit of the doubt to the theory when it fails by acknowledging that there would be a reason for so to happen

### Prospect Theory

Prospect theory says that our choices are not optimal, they are influenced by how our choices are framed around us, i.e., they are context-dependent - these evaluations are choices are made wrt reference points (such as current wealth when talking about gambling). But Prospect Theory is inadequate when it comes to the consideration of disappointment and regret in the evaluation of choices

While people are trying to avoid risks when there are gains to be made, in the scenario where all choices are bad and loss-causing, people tend to be risk-taking

Sensitivity to dimensions reduces as wealth increases: $100 has a different level of satisfaction between people owning $500 and $50,000

Loss aversion is backed by Evolution: Organisms that treat threats as more urgent than opportunities have a better chance to survive and reproduce

**Loss Aversion Ratio:** The gain that people would expect in order to take risk on a loss, which is typically in the range 1.5 - 2.5

Poor people are on the steeper portion of the value curve as they live below their reference points (inability to afford necessities), hence they treat money as reduced losses and not gain

### Endowment Effect

Owning a good makes us value it more than its monetary value. This is typically true for goods which are not regularly traded, such as sold out concert, limited edition sneakers, or a fine bottle of wine

No Endowment Effect is expected when owners view their goods as carriers of value for future exchanges, common in commerce and financial markets

### Bad Events

Bad emotions, stereotypes, feedback, etc leaves a stronger impact compared to good counterparts. For example, whenever terms are renegotiated between two parties, the one who is gaining is inflicting a loss on the other. Since losses are dealt with more intensely as humans tend to dislike losses more, this leads to a disproportionate response to Negotiation. Same is applicable to many other areas where one entity loses and other gains

Because negotiators are influenced by a norm of reciprocity, a concession that is presented as painful calls for an equally painful (and perhaps equally inauthentic) concession from the other side

### Interpretation of chances

- The expectation principle does not correctly describe utility of gambles. For example, for chances of getting $1 million going from 0% → 5% and 95% → 100% increase utility more compared to 30% → 35% and 60% → 65%, even though all are 5% increments
- This effect occurs due to qualitative changes in occurrence instead of just quantitative changes
- 0% → 5% is **Possibility Effect**, something impossible is now slightly possible
- 95% → 100% is **Certainty Effect**, something very probable is now certain

Decision weights to probability are inaccurate. Low probability events are overweighted (Possibility Effect) and high probability events are underweighted compared to certain occurrence (Certainty Effect). The emotional responses are more intense in health related chances compared to financial gains

Effect of frequency format is large. Framing a problem as "1% people die from this virus" v/s "10 in 1000 people die from this virus" have different effects, as the second way of framing creates the images of people suffering contrary to just percentages

### Risk Policies

**Narrow Framing:** The tendency to compare choices between options in isolation and making multiple decisions while not considering the bigger picture

**Broad Framing:** Taking a single decision over all options, considering all possible combinations of options

Broad Framing is better than Narrow Framing. This is so because taking 5 binary choices, Narrow Framing evaluates the 5 choices in isolation, but Broad Framing considers all 32 possible combinations and picks the best one, and hence, it builds rationality

Broad Framing blunts emotional reactions to losses and increases affinity for risks

A risk policy (such as health insurance) is a broad frame as it considers the global statistics of health risks amounting to something significant

### Keeping Score

We keep scores of different things in different mental accounts, which can often lead to Narrow Framing and Disposition Effect

**Disposition Effect** causes an investor to put their stocks in different mental accounts and assess them in isolation, Narrow Framing, and they try to close each account on a profit instead of focusing on the overall portfolio, as a rational decision maker would do

A rational decision maker is interested only in the future consequences of current investments. The decision to invest additional resources in a losing account, when better investments are available, is known as the **Sunk-Cost Fallacy**

Emotional reactions are stronger when some outcome occurs due to action instead of inaction

### Frames and Reality

Not winning a lottery v/s losing a gamble: Bad outcomes become more acceptable when they're framed as a cost of a lottery ticket instead of a loss in a gamble → Cost > Loss. The lost money is associated with the cost of the ticket, but loss of the gamble is associated with the betting itself

##### Brain regions involved in Decision Making
- Amygdala is associated with emotional arousal, and is strongly activated when the choices available conform to the frame
  - Accessed very rapidly by emotional stimuli
  - Likely suspect for activity of System 1
- Anterior Cingulate is activated when a subject expresses self control and conflict
  - Most active when we do something against what comes naturally, such as choosing the sure thing when the gamble is a loss
  - Resisting System 1 involves conflict
- Most rational subjects have a developed frontal area which combines emotions and reasoning to guide decisions
  - Rational individuals didn't show much conflict, rather they were reality-bound and rational choices came naturally

Framing does not intervene with our underlying preferences. Our preferences are subject to framed problems, and our moral intuitions are linked to descriptions, not substance

Different frames are placed in different mental accounts, which lead to different interpretation of losses
- If a pre-purchased ticket to a play is lost, then the lost is attributed in the mental account of the play and the cost appears to have doubled for the same experience
- On the other hand, if someone loses the cash designated for the purchase of the ticket on the spot, then it is put in general revenue account, not the play, and the decision then becomes can the person still afford the play given their reduction in wealth because the cost of the play hasn't doubled

Loss of cash results in rational choice and avoids Sunk-Cost Fallacy

### Two Selves

The actual experience of emotions and the memory of it differ among humans

**Peak-end rule:** The global retrospective rating of emotions experienced during some event are well predicted by the average of the level of pain reported at the worst moment of the experience and at its end

**Duration Neglect:** The duration of the procedure had no effect whatsoever on the ratings of total pain.

For procedures:
- To reduce the memory of pain, make the intensity of peak pain lower and gradually reduce the intensity towards the end
- To reduce the actual pain experienced, a swift procedure is more suitable

Objectively, the pain experienced is integrating the "area under the curve" of a graph that represents momentary pain - this is a **sum** function, something we are not good with

The memory that the remembering self keeps, in contrast, is a representative moment, **strongly influenced by the peak and the end**

Frenchwomen spend less time with kids but enjoy it more as they have access to childcare and don't have to drive them to different things

- More education is associated with greater satisfaction with life, but worse experienced well-being due to higher stress
- Ill health has stronger adverse effect on experienced well-being than on life evaluation

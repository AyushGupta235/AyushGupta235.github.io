### System 1 and System 2

The central framework of the book:

- **System 1** — Operates automatically and quickly, with little effort and no sense of voluntary control. Handles intuition, pattern recognition, and associations. Learns by storing repeated experience in memory.
- **System 2** — Allocates attention to effortful tasks: complex reasoning, focus, computation. Slow, deliberate, and lazy — it'd rather defer to System 1.

System 1 continuously monitors everything and generates assessments without any prompting. System 2 steps in when System 1's output violates expectations or when a task demands real reasoning.

The catch: **System 2 works on memory retrieved by System 1.** So if System 1 has been selectively activating coherent but biased associations, System 2 is working with tainted inputs — and has no awareness that it's happening.

**Ego Depletion:** Will, self-control, and deliberate thought share the same limited budget. After sustained mental effort, intuitive errors become more common. (And glucose reverses this — the nervous system is the body's biggest consumer of glucose.)

### Cognitive Biases

**Availability Heuristic:** We estimate the likelihood of an event based on how easily an example comes to mind — driven by recency, emotional salience, or media coverage, not actual frequency.

People overestimate their individual contribution in group settings. Everyone in the group feels they did more than 50% of the work. Total contributions from all members exceed 100%.

**Anchoring Bias:** When estimating an unknown quantity, people adjust away from an initial anchor number — but stop too early. System 1 builds a worldview consistent with the anchor, biasing System 2's judgment. Random anchors are just as effective as informative ones. First mover advantage in single-issue negotiations is real.

**Halo Effect:** The tendency to like (or dislike) everything about a person, including things you haven't observed, based on a first impression. First impressions shape how subsequent information is interpreted, not just evaluated.

**Representativeness:** Judging probability by similarity to a prototype, while ignoring base rates. The classic case: people rate "Linda is a bank teller and a feminist" as *more* likely than "Linda is a bank teller" — which is logically impossible (the conjunction can't be more probable than either element alone). (**Conjunction Fallacy**)

**WYSIATI — What You See Is All There Is:** System 1 treats the information currently available as if it were complete. It builds the most coherent story possible from what's in front of it, then acts as though that story is the whole truth.

**The Law of Small Numbers:** Extreme outcomes appear more often in small samples simply because there are fewer data points to regress toward the mean. We mistake this randomness for causation and build narratives to explain it.

### Heuristics and Judgment

System 1 assesses through similarity, causality, and associations. It's good at averages and exemplars, poor at sums. This is why people respond more to a single identifiable victim than to statistics about millions — the single face is a prototype; the number is just a count.

**Availability Cascade:** A minor event catches media attention, resonates emotionally, grows into public panic (often exaggerated), and eventually drives large-scale government action — even if the underlying risk is trivial. Combines with **Probability Neglect** (ignoring real base rates in favor of emotional availability) to produce policy that addresses vivid, rare events while ignoring dull, common ones.

**Affect Heuristic:** People's emotional responses to something (liking it vs. fearing it) bleed into their risk and benefit assessments. If shown only the benefits of a technology, people assume the risks are low. The emotional tail wags the rational dog.

On risk policy: experts measure risk in lives or life-years lost — a "count." The public makes finer distinctions (accident vs. voluntary risk-taking, like skiing). The public's richer sense of risk means experts alone shouldn't build policy.

### Jumping to Conclusions

System 1 is gullible. System 2 is lazy and skeptical but easily overwhelmed. When you're tired, depleted, or cognitively busy, System 1 runs unchecked — and System 1 is where most biases live.

When System 1 encounters a word, it activates a spreading web of associated concepts simultaneously — **associative thinking** — most of which never surface in consciousness. This shapes expectations, emotions, and actions before you've deliberately thought about anything.

**Ideomotor Effect:** An idea can prime behavior. Thinking about old age makes people walk slower. The association runs both ways — behavior reinforces the mental state.

**Positive Test Strategy:** System 2, when evaluating a hypothesis, tends to look for confirming evidence rather than disconfirming evidence. This is a feature of deliberate thought that still produces systematic error.

### How Intuition Forms

Intuition = recognition. With enough practice in a **kind learning environment** (where patterns repeat and feedback is fast), expertise becomes genuine intuition. Firefighters and chess grandmasters have real pattern libraries.

But in wicked learning environments — where feedback is delayed, ambiguous, or absent — experience builds confidence without building accuracy. The expert feels certain and is often wrong.

> "Intuition is nothing more and nothing less than recognition." — Herbert Simon

Skilled people in kind environments improve until certain tasks require almost no attention — freeing mental resources for higher-order thinking. Attention is the scarce resource. Anything occupying working memory reduces available cognitive capacity everywhere else.

### What This Means in Practice

- Slow down for important decisions, especially under time pressure or ego depletion
- Ask: what are the base rates? What would have to be true for this to be wrong?
- Use independent sources of evidence — correlated sources give false confidence
- Watch for strong emotional reactions to an idea as a signal to think harder, not as a reason to trust the feeling
- Compliments, enthusiasm, and surface agreement are System 1 responses — they don't mean the underlying logic is sound

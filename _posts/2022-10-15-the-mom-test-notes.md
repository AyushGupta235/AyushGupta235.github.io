> ⚠️ NOTE: A lot of practical knowledge can only be acquired by reading good and bad conversation examples given in the book, this is merely a summary of the key concepts

A business idea can be validated by asking questions that dig deep, but many questions run the risk of causing Cognitive Biases among the users

Bad conversations are worse than no conversations as they give false belief in ideas, causing needless expenditure of resources

#### The Mom Test

- Talk about their life instead of your idea
- Ask about specifics in the past instead of generics or opinions about the future
- Talk less and listen more

Asking any question that involves prediction about the future or the customer's future behaviour is an over-optimistic lie, such as "Would you pay for this when we launch this?" or "Will there be a market for this?" (when someone makes a future promise that they'll buy your product, it's basically useless unless you guarantee a commitment via a pre-order or a contract)

When asking people about their problem, make sure to ask "What are the implications of this [Problem]?" This will help distinguish between "I-am-desperate-to-solve-this-and-will-pay-for-it" and "it-is-a-problem-but-I-manage-around-it" problems, one gets you Sales, other gets you false positives (if they haven't looked for ways to solve their problem, they won't look towards or buy yours)

Watching someone do a task will show you where the problems and inefficiencies really are, not where the customer thinks they are

When using the mom test, it boils down to this: You aren't allowed to tell them what their problem is, and in return, they aren't allowed to tell you what to build. *They own the problem, you own the solution.*

#### Avoiding bad data

When someone likes your product, it's usually useless data. VCs are wrong 95% of the time when investing their own money, how valuable will a random person's opinion be then? Compliments are fool's gold of customer learning

Thinking a meet "went well" is a warning sign. If you catch yourself or your teammates saying something like this, try to get specific

During an interview, when some throws generics or hypothetical fluffs, follow The Mom Test and bring them back to specifics in the past, when it happened, implications, what they did, etc.

> When using generics, people tell who they want to be. Specifics tell who they are. Dig to find edge-cases

When people suggest feature requests, dig around into specifics to find out whether they'll actually use it. Dig around emotional signals the same way, any strong emotion is worth exploring

#### Asking important questions

You should always ask a question that you're terrified of and has the potential to complete destroy your business idea

We should not fish for compliments. Approval of someone respectable is very attractive, but it's useless data. Only the market can tell if your idea is good. You're searching for truth, not trying to be right. Learnings are frustrating but create progress

Asking "What would you say your biggest problem with going to the gym is?" is where the conversation goes horribly wrong. Instead of figuring out whether staying fit is actually a real problem, we're prematurely zooming in on it. Any response we get is going to be dangerously misleading

Same way people could be doing a lot of things for just fun while not having any monetary intent, such as blogging. It's critical to look through this fog. In this case, zoom out to ask about the blog, not its marketing

Premature zoom makes you learn minutia about the trivial problems. You learn about a particular topic, but haven't got a business. Start broad and don't zoom in until you've found a strong signal, both with your whole business and with every conversation

> A lot of pain points that are very clear and give a strong signal often need to be analysed by yourself as solving them can be a product-related challenge as well, i.e., can you build something to solve this? If someone commits to pay given a certain performance threshold is met, then making business is upon your ability to build the product

- **Product risk** — Can I build it? Can I grow it? Will they keep using it?
- **Market risk** — Do they want it? Will they pay? Are there enough of them?

Gaming is a pure product risk problem. You aren't gonna ask people "Do you like having fun?" or anything else. People are going to buy games, that won't change. So it rests fully upon your product whether your game will succeed or not when taken out to the market

Product risk needs quick action on building side if the market risks have been dealt with. Conversations alone cannot validate high product risk ideas

#### Prepare your list of 3

Your 3 questions will be different for each type of person you're talking to. If you have multiple User Persona, have a list for each

Don't feel obliged to repeat questions you already have reliable data on. Pick up where you left off and keep filling in the picture

#### Keeping it casual

Learning about a customer and their problems works better as a quick and casual chat than a long, formal meeting. Even within a formal meeting, you still want to keep it casual if you want non-biased feedback

At their best, these conversations are a pleasure for both parties. You're probably the first person in a long time to be truly interested in the petty annoyances of their day.

Rule of thumb: If it feels like they're doing you a favour by talking to you, it's probably too formal.

#### How long should the meetings be?

Early stage conversations are very fast. The chats grow longer as you move from the early broad questions ("Is this a real problem?") toward more specific product and industry issues ("Which other software do we have to integrate with to close the sale?")

Once the product is mature and the meetings are more sales oriented, block 30+ minutes to discuss goals, needs, and timelines while accommodating inefficiencies of meetings

#### Commitment and advancement

As the product grows, it invites more nefarious compliments. But on the good side, you can seek some advancement in the acquisition funnel or commitment

Upon failing to push for advancement, we identify "zombie leads" - people who keep taking meetings and pushing off decisions, basically your startup got friend-zoned. You can get out of it by pushing them to either advance or reject your startup and it can serve as vital feedback

**Commitment:** They are showing they're serious by giving up something they value such as time, reputation, or money. Strong commitments are paid in multiple currencies, such as getting a paid trial for their whole team, risking time, reputation and money

**Advancement:** They are moving to the next step of your real-world funnel and getting closer to a sale

A meeting is successful only if there is an advancement or commitment, otherwise it was just a time waste.

Always know the next steps. If you have to say, "Let me have a think about that and get back to you," then you've ruined a perfectly good meeting

#### Compliments

Suppose some user compliments you. You can do either of:

- If you are very early stage, you might ask for an introduction to their boss or tech team or the budget-holder so their needs can be fully understood
- If a bit through development, request them to be alpha user and give feedback. If the problem has been hit correctly, they'll jump on the chance

#### Maturing Product

Even when in product-focused sales meetings, try to seek more open-ended advice to figure out what the market wants

Hard-pitching your product gives binary feedback: you either nailed it or not. You may get feedback on specific features, but focusing on the user's problem instead of pitching your solution is the only way to uncover factual insights

People who are not every emotional about your idea mostly like won't be your crazy first customer. Whenever you see the deep emotion, do your utmost to keep that person close. They are the rare, precious fan who will get you through the hard times and turn into your first sale

#### Finding and reaching customers to interview

It seems weird to unexpectedly interview people because you make it seem like one. Carry a casual conversation into discussing their problems, you'd be more interesting than 99% of people they've spoken to

If it's a topic you both care about, find an excuse to talk about it. Your idea never needs to enter the equation and you'll both enjoy the chat

Ways to reach customers:
- Landing page test
- Organise meetups
- Speaking & teaching
- Industry blogging
- Get clever

Try to get people who are just as passionate about the space as you are onto your advisory board - these people are gems

The framing format should have 5 key elements:
1. You're an entrepreneur trying to solve horrible problem X, usher in wonderful vision Y, or fix stagnant industry Z. Don't mention your idea
2. Frame expectations by mentioning what stage you're at and, if it's true, that you don't have anything to sell
3. Show weakness and give them a chance to help by mentioning your specific problem that you're looking for answers on. This will also clarify that you're not a time waster
4. Put them on a pedestal by showing how much they, in particular, can help
5. Ask for help

Or, in shorter form: Vision / Framing / Weakness / Pedestal / Ask

The mnemonic is "Very Few Wizards Properly Ask [for help]."

#### To commute or to call

Calls damage the delicate power dynamic of these conversations. When someone is having a coffee with you, you can chat as friends

On phone or Skype, people try to squeeze in calls between tasks and "get done with this business asap". Also, it's annoying for them to hear "I just wanna chat over a call"

Setting up a call feels like less overhead in the short-term, but that's because you aren't yet able to see all the time-saving and business-saving benefits of in-person relationships with your customers

#### The advisory flip

When looking for advisors, don't go with the mindset of finding a customer otherwise that damages the power dynamics. Remember you're looking for knowledgeable advice from industry experts

Somewhat counterintuitively, the sales-or-advisor switch also puts you firmly in control of the meeting, since now you're evaluating them

#### How many meetings

Keep talking to people until you stop hearing new information

Usually 3-5 conversations if relatively simple industry and focused customer segment

If 10+ conversations and things are all over the map, then customer segment is fuzzy and needs work

#### Choosing your customers

Inconsistent ideas: Not having 20 conversations with a type of customer, but conversations with 20 types of customers - basically like evaluating a dozen of ideas

Speak to industry experts to get better at identifying the customer segmentation via knowledge of taxonomy of the industry

#### Customer slicing

Creating even finer customer segments by slicing them until you find a good enough fit. Talk to customers who are most representative of your User Persona

Rule of thumb: Good customer segments are a who-where pair. If you don't know where to go to find your customers, keep slicing your segment into smaller pieces until you do

#### Running the process

If all customer feedback is stuck in the head of the interviewer, that's a learning bottleneck - disseminate all info to the founding as early as possible with diligent meeting preparation and good note-taking

Notes should be lightweight enough so that they can be reviewed. Notes are useless if you don't look at them

If something can be answered using secondary desk research, do it. Minimise the obvious and speak to customers on what the internet can't tell you

De-risking and validating business >>>> Speed

#### Reviewing

Disseminate learnings to the team asap. Use notes, share exact quotes wherever possible, and keep it unfiltered

Don't outsource customer learning unless you have excellent team dynamics. Otherwise the founder should do it.

#### Whole process in a few points

The process before a batch of conversations:
- If you haven't yet, choose a focused, findable segment
- With your team, decide your big 3 learning goals
- If relevant, decide on ideal next steps and commitments
- If conversations are the right tool, figure out who to talk to
- Create a series of best guesses about what the person cares about
- If a question could be answered via desk research, do that first

During the conversation:
- Frame the conversation
- Keep it casual
- Ask good questions which pass The Mom Test
- Deflect compliments, anchor fluff, and dig beneath signals
- Take good notes
- If relevant, press for commitment and next steps

After a batch of conversations:
- With your team, review your notes and key customer quotes
- If relevant, transfer notes into permanent storage
- Update your beliefs and plans
- Decide on the next 3 big questions

Having a process is valuable, but don't get stuck in it. Sometimes you can just pick up the phone and hack through the knot

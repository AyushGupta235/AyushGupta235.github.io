### The Core Problem

A business idea can be validated by asking the right questions — but most questions trigger cognitive biases that produce false positives. Bad customer conversations are *worse* than no conversations: they create false belief in ideas and cause you to spend real resources on problems nobody actually has.

---

### The Mom Test

Three rules:
1. **Talk about their life, not your idea**
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not opinions about the future**
3. **Talk less, listen more**

Any question that asks about predicted future behavior is useless. "Would you pay for this when we launch?" is a lie the customer tells you to be polite. A future commitment — verbal — is worth nothing unless it's a pre-order or signed contract.

When asking about a problem: always ask **"What are the implications of this problem?"** This separates *I-am-desperate-to-solve-this-and-will-pay* problems from *it's-annoying-but-I-manage-around-it* problems. One gets you sales, the other gets you false positives.

If they haven't already looked for a solution, they won't buy yours.

> You aren't allowed to tell them what their problem is. In return, they aren't allowed to tell you what to build. *They own the problem, you own the solution.*

---

### Avoiding Bad Data

**Compliments are fool's gold.** When someone says they love your idea, that's useless data. VCs are wrong 95% of the time when investing their own money — why would a random person's enthusiasm be more informative?

A meeting that "went well" is a warning sign. When you catch yourself saying this, get specific: what actually happened? What commitment was made?

When someone gives you a generic or hypothetical answer, bring them back to specifics: *When did this happen? What did you do? What were the consequences?*

> When using generics, people tell you who they want to be. Specifics tell you who they are.

Dig into feature requests and emotional signals. Strong emotion is worth exploring — don't just log it and move on.

---

### Asking Important Questions

Ask the question you're most afraid of — the one that could destroy the idea if answered honestly. If you're not asking it, you're not doing customer research, you're fishing for validation.

**Premature zoom** is one of the most common mistakes. Asking "What would you say your biggest problem with going to the gym is?" assumes staying fit is the real problem before you've confirmed it. Zoom out first — ask about the broader context, not the specific feature. Start broad, and don't zoom in until you've found a strong signal.

Some people do things for fun with no intent to pay — blogging, hobbies, community. Don't mistake engagement for a business. Zoom out and ask about the blog itself, not its marketing.

---

### Product Risk vs. Market Risk

- **Market risk:** Do they want it? Will they pay? Are there enough of them?
- **Product risk:** Can I build it? Can I grow it? Will they keep using it?

Gaming is pure product risk — nobody needs to ask "do people want fun?" The market is proven. Whether your game succeeds depends entirely on the product.

Once market risk is resolved, conversations alone can't validate high product risk ideas. You need to build.

---

### How Long, How Many

Early conversations are short — 15–20 minutes. You're answering broad questions: Is this a real problem?

Conversations grow longer as you move toward specific product and integration questions.

Keep talking until you stop hearing new information. Usually 3–5 conversations if the customer segment is focused. If you've had 10+ conversations and insights are all over the map, your customer segment is too broad.

---

### Commitment and Advancement

As your product matures, watch for **zombie leads** — people who keep meeting with you and pushing decisions off. You got friend-zoned. Push them to either advance or give you a clear no. Even a rejection is useful data.

**Commitment** means they're giving up something they value: time, reputation, or money. Strong commitments involve more than one of these — a paid trial that risks all three.

**Advancement** means they're moving to the next step in your funnel.

A meeting is only successful if it ends with an advancement or a commitment. Otherwise it was a pleasant waste of time.

Always know the next step before leaving. "I'll think about it and get back to you" means you ruined a good meeting.

---

### Finding Customers

The best conversations don't feel like interviews. They're casual chats between two people who both care about the same problem.

Rule of thumb: **If it feels like they're doing you a favour by talking to you, it's too formal.**

Framing when approaching someone cold — **Vision / Framing / Weakness / Pedestal / Ask:**
1. You're trying to solve a specific horrible problem
2. You're early and not selling anything
3. You have a specific gap you're looking to fill (show weakness)
4. They in particular can help (put them on a pedestal)
5. Ask for help

In-person beats calls for the same reason coffee beats a Zoom meeting — people are present, not multitasking between tasks.

---

### Customer Segmentation

**Customer slicing:** Keep narrowing your customer segment until you can answer: *where do I find them?* A good customer segment is a who-where pair.

Don't spread 20 conversations across 20 types of customers. That's evaluating a dozen ideas at once, not validating one.

---

### The Process

**Before a batch of conversations:**
- Choose a focused, findable segment
- Decide your top 3 learning goals
- Identify ideal next steps and commitments to seek
- Do desk research first — don't ask customers things Google can tell you

**During:**
- Frame the conversation
- Keep it casual
- Ask questions that pass the Mom Test
- Deflect compliments, anchor specifics, dig beneath signals
- Take notes

**After:**
- Review notes with the team immediately
- Share exact quotes, unfiltered
- Update beliefs and plans
- Decide on the next 3 big questions

> De-risking and validating the business is more important than speed. Having a process is valuable, but don't get so stuck in it that you can't pick up the phone and hack through the knot.
